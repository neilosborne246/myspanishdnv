Spain Digital Nomad Visa vs Italy — which one is right for you?

Both Spain and Italy now offer dedicated remote work visas. A fair, detailed comparison — income thresholds, processing times, tax incentives, infrastructure, and who benefits most from each.

By the My Spanish DNV team

Published: April 2026 Updated: regularly Read time: 9 min Category: Visa Comparisons

Spain and Italy are the two Mediterranean countries that most frequently come up when remote workers consider a European base. Both are now on the map with dedicated digital nomad visas — Spain since 2023, Italy since late 2024. The question "Spain or Italy?" comes up constantly, and the answer is not always obvious.

This article compares them directly across every dimension that matters: income requirements, application process, processing speed, tax incentives, legal infrastructure, city options, cost of living, healthcare, and the path to long-term residency. We run this website and its associated legal service, so Spain is where we operate — but we will give Italy a fair hearing. For some applicants, it is genuinely the right answer.

Spain's Digital Nomad Visa — the established option

Spain's Digital Nomad Visa (DNV) was created under the Startup Law (Ley de Startups) and launched in 2023. By 2026 it is a fully operational, well-documented visa with a clear application pathway, a dedicated processing unit (the UGE — Unidad de Grandes Empresas), a three-year residence permit route, and an established legal profession built around it.

The key facts for 2026:

  • Income threshold: €2,849/month (200% of Spain's 2026 SMI minimum wage)
  • Processing route: Online via UGE (in-country applicants) or Spanish consulate (applying from abroad) — approximately 20 working days via UGE
  • Initial permit: 1-year visa (consulate route) or 3-year residence permit (UGE route)
  • Renewal: Renew for 2 further years; then eligible for long-term EU residence after 5 years continuous legal residence
  • Nationality path: Spanish nationality after 10 years (2 years for Ibero-American nationals)
  • Tax incentive: Beckham Law — 24% flat rate on Spanish-source income up to €600,000 — available to DNV holders who apply via Modelo 149 within 6 months of Social Security registration. Not automatic and not bundled with the DNV application.

Italy's Digital Nomad Visa — the newer entrant

Italy's Visto per Nomadi Digitali (digital nomad visa) was formally introduced in 2024 under Italian immigration law. As of 2026 it is operational but still significantly less mature than Spain's equivalent. The legal and professional infrastructure around Italian digital nomad visa applications is developing, but it is not yet at the level of Spain's ecosystem.

The key facts for 2026:

  • Income threshold: Broadly equivalent to Italy's minimum income benchmark — approximately €2,700/month equivalent in EUR terms, though the precise figure varies and is assessed differently than Spain's straightforward SMI calculation
  • Processing route: Italian consulate only — there is no online equivalent of Spain's UGE for in-country applicants; all applications go through consular channels
  • Processing time: Variable and generally longer than Spain's — the consular route introduces significant variability, with reported processing times of 60–120 days at many consulates
  • Initial permit: 1-year visa with residence permit conversion on arrival; renewal possible
  • Tax incentive: Italy has a reduced-tax regime for incoming workers (the regime impatriati), but it has been significantly scaled back by 2024 reforms and is now less generous than it was. It does not offer a comparable benefit to Spain's Beckham Law for most digital nomads.

The tax incentive: Beckham Law vs Italy's impatriati regime

Tax is often the deciding factor for higher-earning remote workers. Let's be direct about the comparison.

Spain's Beckham Law offers a flat 24% rate on Spanish-source employment income up to €600,000. For a remote worker earning €80,000/year, this is a very significant saving compared to Spain's standard IRPF rates (which would reach 37–45% at that income level). Beckham Law is specifically available to Digital Nomad Visa holders and has been confirmed under the 2023 Startup Law framework. The catch: it is not automatic, must be applied for within 6 months of Social Security registration via Modelo 149, and is a separate service from the DNV application itself.

Italy's impatriati regime was once similarly attractive — a 70% income exemption at its most generous, meaning only 30% of income was taxable. However, 2024 reforms significantly reduced the benefit. The current regime requires a minimum 2-year residence outside Italy before relocation and offers a 50% income exemption for 5 years (down from previous levels), with conditions attached. For many digital nomads considering Italy in 2026, the tax incentive is materially less generous than Spain's equivalent.

On tax: get country-specific advice

Neither Beckham Law nor Italy's impatriati regime suits every income structure. Both should be assessed by a qualified tax adviser before you make a country decision based primarily on tax. What looks like an obvious win on a comparison table can look different when your specific income sources are factored in.

Application process: where the gap is largest

The single most significant practical difference between the two visas in 2026 is the application process — and specifically the processing time and certainty.

Spain's UGE online route processes applications in approximately 20 working days. The criteria are published, the precedent is extensive, and the professional infrastructure around Spain's DNV is mature. A well-prepared application submitted through a qualified Spanish immigration lawyer has a predictable outcome within a predictable timeframe.

Italy's digital nomad visa still relies entirely on the consular route. Consulates in different countries process applications at different speeds, apply criteria with varying levels of consistency, and the overall volume of Italian digital nomad visa applications is still low enough that individual consular officers have significant discretion. Reported processing times range from two months to four months or more. The rejection rate is harder to assess because precedent is limited — but that cuts both ways.

For someone who needs to plan a move with confidence — who has a lease to sign, a family to relocate, or a business timeline to meet — Spain's certainty is a meaningful practical advantage.

Who should choose Spain

Spain is the stronger choice for the majority of applicants comparing these two visas. Specifically, choose Spain if:

Choose Spain if…
  • Your income is above €2,849/month (you comfortably meet the threshold)
  • You want speed and certainty — the UGE processes in ~20 working days
  • You want access to Beckham Law's 24% flat rate tax incentive
  • You want a well-established legal framework with clear precedent
  • You are targeting Barcelona, Madrid, Málaga, Valencia, or Seville
  • You want a 3-year initial permit rather than starting with 1 year
  • You are an Ibero-American national and want an accelerated nationality path (2 years)
Italy might suit you if…
  • Your income falls slightly below Spain's €2,849/month threshold but above Italy's lower benchmark
  • You have a strong personal or cultural connection to Italy specifically
  • You are targeting Rome, Milan, Florence, or Bologna for lifestyle reasons
  • You are willing to accept longer processing times and more consular variability
  • You have previously lived in Italy and already have social/professional connections there

Cities, lifestyle, and cost of living

Both countries offer genuinely excellent cities for remote workers, with high quality of life, good connectivity, and vibrant international communities. The decision here is often personal rather than objective.

Spain's main cities for digital nomads

Barcelona is the most international and has the largest established digital nomad community — coworking spaces, expat events, and a cosmopolitan English-friendly culture. It is also Spain's most expensive city after Madrid. Madrid is Spain's capital and cultural centre — slightly less tourist-focused than Barcelona, with excellent infrastructure and a growing tech scene. Málaga has become one of Europe's most popular digital nomad destinations — significantly cheaper than Madrid or Barcelona, a good climate, beach proximity, and a fast-growing international community. Valencia and Seville offer lower costs, high quality of life, and growing expat populations without the tourist density of the larger cities.

Italy's main cities

Milan is Italy's financial and design capital — expensive, high-energy, and excellent for business connectivity. Rome is culturally unparalleled but can be chaotic, with bureaucracy that makes Spain look streamlined. Florence is beautiful, smaller, and well-suited to a quieter pace. Bologna is a growing option — a university city with good food, affordable housing, and a lively atmosphere. Southern Italy — Puglia, Sicily, Calabria — is exceptionally affordable and has been promoted through various Italian programmes, though digital infrastructure varies.

Healthcare

Both Spain and Italy operate national health systems that provide residents with access to free or subsidised public healthcare. As a Digital Nomad Visa holder in Spain, you will have access to the SNS (Sistema Nacional de Salud) once you are registered. In Italy, access to the SSN (Servizio Sanitario Nazionale) follows a similar registration process. In both countries, many digital nomads maintain private health insurance — partly for language comfort, partly for access to better facilities, and partly because private health insurance is a requirement during the visa application process in any case.

The legal infrastructure gap — and why it matters

One factor that does not appear in comparison tables but matters enormously in practice is the maturity of the legal profession servicing each visa. Spain's DNV has three years of case history, published guidance, judicial interpretations, and hundreds of immigration lawyers who have processed thousands of applications. When an unusual situation arises — a complex income structure, a rejection, a timeline extension — there is a profession that knows how to handle it.

Italy's digital nomad visa is new enough that this infrastructure does not yet exist in the same way. There are fewer specialists, less precedent, and more situations that require interpretation rather than established practice. For a straightforward case this may not matter. For anything complex, it increases uncertainty.

This is not a criticism of Italy's visa — it is the natural state of a new legal framework. Spain's DNV was also relatively immature in 2023. But in 2026, Spain is three years ahead.
