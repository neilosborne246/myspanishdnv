Spain's DNV vs Portugal's D8 visa — which is actually the better choice in 2026?

Both are EU countries, both have digital nomad visas, both are popular. Here's the honest comparison.

By the My Spanish DNV team

Published: April 2026 Updated: regularly Read time: 8 min Category: Tax & Finance

Spain and Portugal are the two most popular digital nomad visa destinations in Western Europe, and they get compared constantly. Both are Iberian Peninsula countries with warm climates, excellent food, manageable costs relative to northern Europe, and well-developed expat communities. Both are EU Schengen members. Both have dedicated digital nomad visa programmes.

So which one should you choose? The honest answer is: it depends — but there are some clear objective differences that most people weigh up, and we'll work through each of them here. We operate in Spain, so we have an obvious position, but we've tried to be genuinely balanced in this comparison. If Portugal is genuinely the better fit for you, you should know that.

Income requirements: Spain is slightly lower

Spain's 2026 income minimum is €2,849/month — 200% of Spain's SMI. Portugal's D8 income minimum is set at four times the Portuguese national minimum wage, which works out at approximately €3,040/month or above for a solo applicant in the current period. The figures are comparable, with Spain slightly lower.

In practice, most remote workers considering either country will exceed both thresholds. The difference is unlikely to be the deciding factor for most people. But if you're right on the margin, Spain's slightly lower threshold is relevant.

Processing time: Spain wins clearly

This is the most significant practical difference between the two visa programmes in 2026. Spain's UGE route processes applications in approximately 20 working days — roughly four calendar weeks. Portugal's D8 is processed via the consulate route and typically takes three to six months.

That's not a marginal difference — it's a structurally different experience. An applicant choosing Spain on the UGE route can be in Spain with a permit in approximately 10–12 weeks total. An applicant going through Portugal's D8 process should plan for six months from starting to arriving with a legal status resolved.

For people with a firm departure date or notice period running out, this is often the deciding factor. Spain is simply faster.

Tax regimes: Beckham Law vs the reformed NHR/IFICI

This section requires careful attention because the situation in Portugal changed significantly in 2024 — and some of the information circulating online about Portugal's tax advantages reflects the old regime, not the current one.

Spain's Beckham Law

Spain's Beckham Law (Régimen de Impatriados) taxes qualifying employed workers at a flat 24% rate on income up to €600,000 for the first year plus five additional years. Qualifying conditions: you must apply within six months of Social Security registration, must not have been Spanish tax resident in the preceding five years, and must be employed under an employment contract (not self-employed). For employed digital nomads, Beckham Law is consistently accessible and well understood. It is a separate application from the DNV — handled by a Spanish tax adviser.

Portugal's NHR / IFICI regime

Portugal's Non-Habitual Resident (NHR) regime was historically one of the most attractive tax regimes in Europe for digital nomads — a flat 20% rate on Portuguese-source income and 0% on foreign-source income for 10 years. This made Portugal extremely attractive for self-employed workers and those with foreign income sources.

However, in 2024, Portugal significantly reformed the NHR regime. The reformed regime — renamed IFICI (Incentivo Fiscal à Investigação Científica e Inovação) — is now specifically targeted at qualifying professionals in priority sectors: scientific research, technology, innovation, and certain high-value-added activities. It is no longer broadly available to digital nomads regardless of sector. A UK accountant, a project manager, a marketing consultant, or a designer working remotely may no longer qualify for IFICI where they would previously have qualified for NHR.

Without IFICI, standard Portuguese IRS (income tax) rates apply — progressive rates reaching approximately 48% on the highest incomes. This is broadly comparable to Spain's standard IRPF without Beckham Law.

The upshot for 2026: Spain's Beckham Law is currently more consistently accessible for employed digital nomads than Portugal's IFICI. If you work in tech, research, or innovation, Portugal's IFICI may still apply — check with a Portuguese tax adviser. For everyone else, Spain's Beckham Law is the cleaner tax advantage.

💡

Tax advice is country-specific

If you're seriously considering Portugal, get a current assessment from a Portuguese tax adviser on whether IFICI applies to your profession and income type. The regime changed in 2024 and some older online resources still describe the original NHR terms. Don't assume Portugal's tax advantage applies to you without confirming it with current advice.

Lifestyle comparison: an honest look at both countries

Tax and processing time aside, the choice between Spain and Portugal is often ultimately a lifestyle decision. We encourage honest assessment of what each country actually feels like to live in.

Spain

Spain is significantly larger — more diverse landscapes, more cities, more options. Barcelona is Mediterranean, cosmopolitan, and well-connected internationally. Madrid is a dynamic capital with strong business infrastructure. Málaga has become one of Europe's fastest-growing digital nomad hubs — a combination of good weather, lower costs than Barcelona, and direct international flights. Valencia, Seville, San Sebastián, Bilbao — Spain has depth that Portugal simply can't match geographically. Infrastructure is strong. Healthcare is excellent. The pace of life varies enormously by city.

Portugal

Portugal is smaller — which means it's more concentrated but also more intimate. Lisbon has become a genuine European tech hub with a strong startup scene and a warm, accessible city culture. Porto is beloved by people who find Lisbon too frenetic. The Algarve is popular for those who want beach-adjacent life at a gentler pace. English is extremely widely spoken in Lisbon and Porto — arguably more so than equivalent Spanish cities among the younger population. The cost of living in Lisbon has risen significantly over the past decade and is now comparable to Barcelona in many respects. Outside Lisbon and Porto, Portugal is significantly cheaper.

Both countries have long Atlantic coastlines, excellent food, warm people, and genuine quality of life. Neither is a bad choice.

The honest recommendation

For most employed digital nomads considering a move to the Iberian Peninsula in 2026, Spain's DNV is the stronger practical choice on the objective criteria: faster processing, slightly lower income threshold, and a more consistently accessible tax advantage via Beckham Law.

The case for Portugal is strongest if: you specifically love Portugal and have a clear preference for Lisbon or Porto; you work in a sector that qualifies for IFICI; or you have Portuguese ancestry or family connections that make Portugal a natural home.

If you're genuinely torn and purely choosing on practical grounds: Spain is currently easier, faster, and — for employed workers — has a clearer tax advantage. If you've visited both and have a strong preference for Portugal: choose Portugal. The visa follows the life you want, not the other way around.
